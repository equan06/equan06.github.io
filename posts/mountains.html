Why do I Climb Mountains?

Many people ask me why I climb mountains. Some might say that it’s for the achievement; anyone who climbs Mt. Whitney gets to brag that they’ve climbed the highest mountain in the lower 48. Why else do so many people spend thousands to climb Everest, or Denali, or any of the other seven summits?

I don’t really relate, partly because I almost rarely talk about my mountaineering with anyone. I tell people occasionally that I climb mountains for fun, and that’s about it. Most of my mountaineering is done solo and isn’t posted anywhere on the internet. Plus, the vast majority of the mountains that I climb aren’t notable to the average person, so there’s not much to brag about. I mean, how many people have ever heard of Mt. Morrison or Black Kaweah?

If you’ve never climbed a difficult mountain (for discussion’s sake, anything >3,000 ft of elevation gain above 10,000 ft), you might not realize how physically and mentally strenuous it is to lug a pack up a mountain, barely fueled by poor sleep and questionable nutrition (my setup includes an abundance of potato chips and sugary energy bars). If I’m in bad shape, I’ll need to stop every minute or so to take a breather and let my heart rate stabilize. And in those brief periods of respite from the gruelling physical agony, when my leg muscles are burning, my lungs are begging for air, and my cheeks are getting whipped by high speed gusts, I start to really question my sanity.

But it’s not always that bad. Climbing remote peaks in the Sierras takes an incredible amount of preparation across many dimensions, and when you’re well prepared, it can actually be quite an enjoyable and engaging experience. You often have to backpack through the rugged wilderness before even reaching anything remotely technical, thus requiring a variety of outdoors skills to keep yourself alive. The actual climb itself requires studying the route adeptly: turn left at this landmark, follow the leftmost chute and avoid this other chute, cross the glacier and climb this specific set of rocks, etc. Sometimes you’ll run into obstacles in the way, such as unexpected bad weather, incorrect route information, dangerous terrain, or a lack of physical preparation, and you need to have enough experience to know when to adjust the plan or retreat and come back another day.

It’s basically a sort of flow state. When I’m in the moment and making quick decisions subconsciously on how to continue safely and efficiently, I find myself excited to keep trudging on despite the fatigue. It’s this unique combination of decision making, technical, physical, and mental coping skills that keeps every climb interesting; every new peak is a new adventure and chance to learn something new about yourself, regardless of whether you actually succeed in climbing it.

Climbing mountains also gives me a greater appreciation of the comforts of modern life. I believe that life is best experienced with contrasts; you appreciate the good more after having experienced the bad. If you never experience the cold, how can you appreciate the comfort of warmth?

Climbing a mountain is risky, grueling, and often tedious. I’ve only turned around twice out of the dozen or so peaks I’ve attempted; both were due to a lack of physical preparation and not willing to take the risk of climbing a route that was sketchier than expected. However as I reflect back upon these “failures,” I realize now that they were chances for me to learn more about my own style of risk management; how I deal with uncertainty and risk tolerance. And they made every subsequent success feel just a little more deserved.

It’s also a reminder on how life can sometimes be objectively brutal and unforgiving, no matter how prepared you are. There are many things you can do to increase your chance of having a successful climb; improving your fitness, choosing the right gear, doing enough research, selecting the right partners. But the mountain doesn’t care about your litany of excuses, no matter how valid they are. It might throw a hail storm at you, or a bunch of rocks come tumbling down to spook you away, or maybe it ends up just being way sketchier than you could have imagined. Whatever the adversity, you’re forced to suck it up and deal with it all the same, just like the last person who either got to the summit or didn’t. There’s really no shortcuts here, barring the edge cases of people paying guides or porters to do most of the work for them.

Ultimately, I’m lucky and privileged enough that I get to choose many of my own challenges. I see mountaineering as a sort of training ground for dealing with life. So that when the time comes, I have the confidence to pull from my unique set of experiences and skills to make the best decisions that I can under pressure.
